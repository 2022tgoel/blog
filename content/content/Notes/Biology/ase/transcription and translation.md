
* **TATA Box**: A promotor sequence, present in eukaryotes. around 35 codons before the start of the transcription site. 
* **CpG** islands can also indicate the start of transcription. Methylation of CpG islands toggles gene expression. 
* **transcription factors**: they find the promoter and bind to it. transcription initiation complex. 
* **RNA polymerase** is the main workhorse. Transcription starts with the 5' end of the RNA and goes to the 3' end. So, dna is reads in the opposite direciton
* In RNA, T is substituted for U. The difference in this is a methyl group. Not having the methyl group for RNA makes it cheaper but also less stable. Uracil's presence also helps distinguish RNA from DNA and allows for quick degradation when no longer needed.
* **terminator**: for prokaryotes, the end of transcription
	* At the end of transcription, a **GC-rich sequence** (an Inverted Terminal Repeat, or ITR) in the DNA template creates a self-complementary region in the nascent RNA, forming a stable **hairpin loop**, often followed by a weak stretch of uracils (U's), causing the RNA polymerase to stall and detach, thus stopping transcription. This "CG" (Cytosine-Guanine) rich hairpin, coupled with the weak A-U bonds, destabilizes the RNA-DNA hybrid, releasing the new RNA molecule.
* In eukaryotes, the hairpin would interfere with RNA post-processing. 
	* **Polyadenylation signal**: (abbreviated to poly-A signal) The RNA contains **AAUAAA**, which is recognized by cleavage/polyA factors. Polymerase keeps going. 
	* **5' cap** and **poly-A tail**. 
	* There is also **splicing** carried out after by a **splicesosome** 
* **histones**: these positively charged things that DNA wrap around. Makes it 
	* Positive charge from lysine and argenine. adding acetyl groups makes these less positively charged (neutralizes lysine), so DNA wraps around less tightly 
* **nuclesomes**: collection of histones + DNA
* **Ribosome** sites
	* E: exit
	* P: peptide something. assembling the chain.
	* A: amino something. tRNA enters here. 
* DNA polymerase’s active site can’t stabilize the very first nucleotide pair well enough to start from scratch, thus needing a primer, while RNA polymerase has special features that _do_ stabilize the first 1–2 nucleotide, so it can begin **de novo**
* **exonucleases** chew up dna from the ends. rna needs to be protected from this.
