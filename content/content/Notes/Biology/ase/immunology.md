* B-cell antigen receptor = antibody = immunoglobulin (Ig)
	* Symmetric polypeptide chains, heavy chain and light chain
		* both membrane-bound and can be secreted later
	* three complementarity determining regions (CDRs) -- high variablility regions of the polypeptide chain
	* Each B-cell expresses one and only one antibody, and it has unique antigen
	* **somatic recombination** of the genes generate diversity for the antibodies (you don't have enough real estate in the genome itself to encode all of them)
* T-cell receptor (TCR)
	* always membrane bound
	* just two chains of polypeptides

* antigen presentation on the surface of the cell to indicate what is going on inside the cell
* Major Histocompatibility Complex (MCH)
	* class 1 MCH are displayed in all nucleated cells
		* The peptides held by are are from the cytosol



| Class 1 MCH                 | Class 2 MCH                             |
| --------------------------- | --------------------------------------- |
| All nucleated cells have it | B-cells                                 |
| CD8 T cells                 | CD4 T Cells                             |
| peptide attached in ER      | peptide attached in the vesicle         |
| peptide comes from cytosol  | peptide comes from extracellular matrix |
* CD8 / CD4 are *co-receptors* that are for recognizing specific MHC
* CD8+ T-cells are killer/cytotoxic T-Cells
* CD4+ T-cells enhance B-cell function
	* they meet and collaborate in the lymph nodes
* **isotype switching**
* So a b-cell can recognize a certain type of antigen. It displays the type of antigen that it recognizes on the surface through MHC II. Then the T-cell can recognize the peptide, and this will trigger downstream effects like affinity maturation, the creation of memory B-cells , etc.

##### How does the immune system distinguish between self and foreign
1. during development in the lymph nodes, if it detects anything it is self
2. Ag + innate immune response --> foriegn
3. Ag only --> self

* Even non-live vaccines can get antigens inside cells, because these phagocytes eat up the bacteria, and then present them on MCH II

### Memory
These antibodies have memory, such that
1. The lag to produce antibodies is shorter
2. the concentration is larger
3. the fit (affinity) of the antibodies is stronger
the second time you generate an immune response to a foreign substance.


## Antibiotics

- They bind to penicillin-binding proteins (PBPs) in bacteria
- PBPs normally cross-link peptidoglycan, which gives the bacterial cell wall strength
- Without a proper wall, the bacterium bursts (lysis), especially while dividing
