* autosomal genes appear on chromosomes 1-22
* **sex-linked** genes appear on the sex chromosome (23)
	* In this case, it you have an X-linked, and the father is dominant, all the daughters will the dominant trait, because they will inherit the dominant allele from the father. 
	* The human Y chromosome is small and gene-poor, but it carries genes that are crucial for male development and fertility. The Y chromosome exists mainly to say to make testes and make sperm, almost nothing else

* Linkage -- genes are physically near each other on the chromosome. Then they are likely to be inherited together. However, **crossing-over** / **recombination** can still cause them not be inherited, resulting in a **non-parental gamete** (cell with a chromosome with different set of alleles than what is found in any parent chromosome)
	* recombination frequency $\propto$ distance between genes on the chromosome. 
* A **three-point cross** can be used to determine the distance between genes on the same chromosome. 

* What's great about yeast is that the product of a single meiotic division (the 4 haploid cells) are bundled together which means that you can look at it products of a single meiosis. 
	* Suposed you have two genes, of different chromosomes, and a dominant and recessive version of both (A, a, B, and b)
	* Then if there is no recombination, after meiosis 1, the chromosome copies in a single cell are identical. Therefore, the resulting haploid cells are identical. This means you can only get two different versions of the cell, which are the parental ditype and non-parental ditype. This is (AB AB ab ab) and (Ab Ab aB aB)
	* However, if you do have a recombination event somewhere between the gene and the centromore, then you can get the *tetratype* (AB Ab aB ab)
#### Cell Division

- **Mitosis:** A single cell division that produces two genetically identical daughter cells with the same number of chromosomes as the parent cell. 
- **Meiosis:** Two rounds of cell division that produce four genetically unique daughter cells (gametes) with half the number of chromosomes. During an extended G2 phase (meiotic prophase), homologous chromosomes link up with each other and undergo genetic recombination. In meiosis I, you get a random half of the chromosomes, each with different combinations of of the genetic material in the other chromosome. Then, in the second phase they are split into unpaired chromosomes similar to mitosis.
